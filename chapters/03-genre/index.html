The Genre Desertification.

Have you heard that the first heavy metal band was not inducted until 2006? Black Sabbath, the genre's founding act, waited 31 years after the Hall opened. For two decades, entire genres were invisible. The data shows exactly which ones, and exactly when they disappeared.

Methodology

What this chapter does: We measure genre diversity across 40 years of Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Performer-category inductions (1986-2026). Each inducted artist is classified into one of ten genre categories. Two metrics quantify the pattern: a per-genre Bias Score and Simpson's Diversity Index per year and per era.

Genre Bias Score: For each genre, we compute its share of inductions within an era (Wenner: 1986-2019, post-Wenner: 2020-2026) and divide by its overall historical share. A score of 1.0 means proportional representation. Below 1.0 means the genre was underrepresented in that era. Above 1.0 means overrepresentation.

Simpson's Diversity Index: Computed as 1 minus the sum of squared genre proportions for each year. Ranges from 0 (all inductees in one genre) to approaching 1 (inductees evenly distributed across genres). We report per-year values and era aggregates.

Data sources: Rock and Roll Hall of Fame official inductee database. Genre classifications derived from primary biographical sources and MusicBrainz tags, reviewed by Agent Rosetta.

What this chapter cannot show: Genre underrepresentation documents an outcome. It does not prove that genre was a deliberate selection criterion. Nominating committee composition, voter familiarity, and genre boundary definitions all contribute to these patterns. The reader draws conclusions.
